Ever open your notes and realize every idea looks like the last one? You are not out of ideas. You are stuck in the same thought patterns.

This post shows you how to hand AI the role of devil's advocate so it can shake up your planning from the outside.

**What you will learn**

- Why solo brainstorming stalls and how an outside perspective helps
- A prompt template that tells AI to argue back, not just agree
- A before-and-after walkthrough for a "viewer quiz stream" concept

---

## Why solo brainstorming keeps producing look-alikes

When you brainstorm alone, your habits take over. Three patterns show up a lot.

1. **You keep remixing past successes** — you lean on what worked before and only tweak the surface.
2. **You default to your favorite genres** — the games and topics you love may not match what viewers want.
3. **You filter too early** — you shoot down bold ideas before they have a chance to grow, because you worry about feasibility.

All three come from your own internal filters. The fix is simple: talk to someone with a different perspective. But a co-creator is not always around.

That is where AI helps. It has no track record, no favorite genre, and no ego. So it can challenge your assumptions without being pulled into the same ruts.

---

## The devil's-advocate prompt template

If you treat AI as a plain idea generator, you get safe, generic suggestions. The trick is to tell it to **disagree and reframe**.

### Base prompt

```
You are the devil's advocate for my stream ideas.
For each idea I give you, do these three things:

1. Point out the weakest part of the concept.
2. Suggest one different angle or approach.
3. Propose one way to make it interactive for viewers.

Stream idea: [write your idea here]
```

Three things make this work.

- **"Weakest part"** forces AI to spot blind spots you might miss.
- **"Different angle"** pushes the idea in directions you would not think of.
- **"Interactive for viewers"** grounds everything in what works on a live stream.

Once AI pushes back, follow up with "how would you fix that?" Each round digs deeper.

## Polish an idea in three steps

Here is a simple workflow for turning a rough thought into a ready-to-run plan.

### Step 1: Plant the seed

Drop your raw idea into the prompt. It does not have to be polished. Something like "I want to do a viewer quiz stream" is enough.

**Tip:** Add one constraint or goal, such as "for a chat stream" or "to attract new viewers." It keeps AI's answers focused.

### Step 2: Twist it

Read AI's feedback, then rewrite the idea based on the weak points and new angles it raised. The key rule: **do not copy-paste AI's suggestions**. Treat them as raw material and blend them with your own style.

Pick the parts that feel like you and add them to your notes.

**Tip:** You can ask AI to "make this idea more X" to steer it closer to your stream's vibe.

### Step 3: Build an action plan

Ask AI to list five concrete prep steps for the idea. That turns a vague concept into something you can act on right away.

By the end of this step you have a plan, not just a memo.

## Walkthrough: "Viewer Quiz Stream" before and after

Let's see the process in action. The topic: a viewer-participation quiz stream.

### Before: solo brainstorm

> - Do a quiz stream
> - I will write the questions myself
> - Award points for correct answers
> - Post a monthly leaderboard

Solid, but plenty of streamers do the same thing. There is nothing that sets this apart.

### After: a few rounds with AI

AI's pushback: "Writing all the questions yourself sounds like a lot of prep. What if viewers submit the questions instead?"

That one comment flips the concept.

> - **Viewers submit questions, the streamer answers on the spot**
> - Viewers post questions in chat; the streamer answers off the cuff
> - Rotate a genre restriction each session (anime this week, cooking next week)
> - Wrong answers trigger a mini penalty-game segment
> - Award an "MVP" to the most entertaining question, regardless of difficulty

Then AI generated a prep list:

1. Pick genre themes for the next four sessions
2. Prepare five penalty-game options
3. Write a teaser tweet announcing the first session's genre
4. Create a question template viewers can follow (e.g., "If you had to name one X, what would it be?")
5. Decide how to display the MVP on stream (text overlay, sound effect, etc.)

Compared with the original, the roles are reversed and the prep load is lighter. The AI's pushback exposed the hidden assumption that the streamer has to write every question.

## Wrap-up

- Solo brainstorming stalls because your own habits filter out fresh directions. AI has none of those habits, so it works as a neutral outside voice.
- The prompt template ("point out the weak spot," "suggest a different angle," "make it interactive") keeps AI from giving safe, forgettable answers.
- Three steps — plant the seed, twist it, build an action plan — turn a rough note into something you can run with.

### Try it tonight

1. Paste the base prompt into your AI tool of choice.
2. Type one line about the next stream idea you want to develop.
3. Rewrite one part of your idea based on the feedback.

Five minutes is enough. You will probably spot at least one assumption you didn't realize you were making.
