"I want to start doing game commentary, but I'm not sure which game to pick." Most of that uncertainty comes from one place: copyright. Commercial titles have per-game streaming policies — some allow it, some don't, some have complicated conditions around monetization or spoilers.

Free games cut through most of that. The permission process is cleaner, the cost is zero, and a lot of them are short enough to finish in a single stream. That makes them ideal for a first commentary stream.

**What you'll learn:**
- Why free games reduce risk on both the legal and financial side
- Three platforms for finding them and how to confirm permissions
- How to pick a game that actually makes for an engaging stream

---

## Why Free Games Work Well for Your First Stream

### Permissions are more straightforward

Commercial game streaming policies vary wildly — one title might restrict monetization, another might ban showing certain story scenes, another might require you to stop once you reach a certain point. You have to check each one individually.

Free games typically state their streaming permissions clearly on the game's page, or the creator is accessible enough to ask directly. The answer is usually just "yes" or "not without asking."

That said: "free" doesn't automatically mean "okay to stream." Some free games explicitly prohibit streaming or archiving. Always check before you go live.

### Zero upfront cost

Buying a commercial title before you know whether you'll enjoy streaming it is a real risk for someone just starting out. Free games let you figure out what kind of game works for you — and what kind of commentary style you want — without spending money.

### You can finish it in one stream

Most free games clock in at 1–4 hours. "I started it and I'll finish it tonight" is a compelling setup for viewers. Shorter games also remove the momentum problem that can sink a long RPG series after episode three.

## Three Platforms for Finding Free Games

### Freem! (Japanese games)

Japan's largest free game distribution site, with strong representation in horror, visual novel, and RPG genres. Game pages often list streaming/recording permissions explicitly.

Permission check steps:
1. Open the game's page
2. Look for a "terms of use" or "streaming/video" section
3. If nothing is listed, contact the creator via X (formerly Twitter) or the game page's contact form

### itch.io (international)

Strong English-language selection across all genres. Check the game's comments section — creators often post "feel free to stream!" or similar notes there. A Creative Commons or Public Domain license in the License field generally covers streaming, but double-check commercial use if you're monetized.

### PLiCy (browser games)

Browser-based games you can capture directly in OBS without installing anything. Great for very early setups where you want to minimize technical complexity.

### 3-step permission check

```
1. Read the game page's terms / README
2. If "streaming OK" or "let's play welcome" — you're good
3. If nothing is listed, send one message to the creator
   (No reply = don't stream it)
```

Template message:
```
Hi, I'm [streaming name] and I'd like to do a live commentary
stream of "[game title]," including keeping the archive public.
Is that okay? Thanks for your time.
```

## Picking a Game That Makes for a Good Stream

Clearing permissions is the first step. The second is finding a game your audience will actually enjoy watching.

### Criterion 1 — Emotional range

Does the game make you feel things? Scared, surprised, amused, moved? Reactions are the engine of commentary streams. A quiet puzzle game where everything goes smoothly is harder to watch than one with swings in tension and energy.

Horror, mystery visual novels, and comedic RPGs all naturally generate emotional range. That's why they're overrepresented in commentary streaming.

### Criterion 2 — Completable in 1–3 hours

For a first stream, completing the game on stream is worth prioritizing. It gives the stream a clear arc, prevents the slow energy drain of a session that goes longer than expected, and leaves you with something concrete to look back on.

### Criterion 3 — Something on screen

A game with characters, backgrounds, and animation gives viewers something to look at even during quieter moments. Text-heavy visual novels aren't a bad choice — read-aloud commentary can work great — but be aware the visual experience is different.

### Genre quick reference

| Genre | Emotional range | Length | Visual |
|---|---|---|---|
| Short horror | High | 1–2 hrs | Variable |
| Comedic RPG | Medium-high | 2–3 hrs | Good |
| Mystery VN | Medium | 2–4 hrs | Good |
| Pure text VN | Lower | Varies | Minimal |

## A First-Stream Structure That Works

```
[Open – 15 min]
- Intro and self-introduction (longer if it's a debut stream)
- Game introduction: what is this, why did I pick it?

[15 min – 15 min before the end]
- Play with commentary, pick up chat reactions
- Periodic "here's where we are in the story" updates

[Last 15 min]
- Finish the game and share honest reactions
- What surprised me, what was hard, what I'd recommend
- What's next / next stream announcement
```

The post-game reaction section is where personality comes through most clearly. Talk through the high points, the parts that were difficult, and anything that surprised you. That's where viewers decide whether they want to come back.

---

## Keeping the Momentum Going

Once you've found a format that works, having a "next game" ready before you finish the current one prevents the dead zone between streams.

- After each stream, pick 1–2 candidates for the next one
- Get permissions confirmed before you need them
- Alternating short games (1–2 hrs) with slightly longer ones (3–4 hrs) keeps the schedule feeling varied

---

## Recap

- **Free games lower both the legal and financial risk for a first commentary stream** — permissions are usually straightforward, and you don't need to buy anything before knowing whether the format works for you
- **Freem!, itch.io, and PLiCy cover most of what you need** — check permissions with a simple 3-step process, and send one message if nothing is listed
- **Emotional range, 1–3 hour length, and good visuals are the three things to screen for** — pick games that hit at least two out of three and you'll avoid the flat-stream trap
