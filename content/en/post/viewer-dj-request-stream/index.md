Ever wanted your viewers to pick what you sing on stream? Request streams are a simple interactive format that naturally drives up comment count. Add a voting round on top of plain requests and the energy goes even higher.

This article walks through the core mechanics of a request stream, a three-phase run-of-show template, and how to choose the right collection method based on your audience size.

## What Is a Request Stream? An Interactive Format That Can Multiply Your Chat

A request stream lets viewers submit songs or topics, and you respond to them live. Pair it with a singing stream (often called an *utawaku* in the VTuber community) and your audience decides what you perform. Even in a zatsudan (chat-focused) stream, you can ask viewers to submit the next discussion topic.

The key driver is anticipation — viewers keep commenting because their pick might get chosen. Trying this once can push your chat volume to 1.5 to 2 times your usual numbers.

Here are three patterns you can try:

1. **Singing Request** — Collect about 10 song suggestions from chat, then perform the top 5.
2. **BGM Request** — Each viewer proposes a track for your work-session BGM.
3. **Topic Request** — Gather five topics from chat and use a random wheel to pick the next one.

## Run-of-Show Template: Collect, Vote, Perform

Splitting your stream into three phases keeps things smooth even on your first try. Set a rough time budget for each phase so the pacing stays tight. For a 60-minute stream, try 15 minutes collecting, 10 minutes voting, and 35 minutes performing.

### Phase 1: Collect Requests (Start through Minute 15)

Open the stream by announcing that you are taking requests right now. Share any ground rules up front — for example, "J-POP only" or "songs under five minutes." The first 15 minutes, while chat is still settling in, is prime collection time.

### Phase 2: Vote and Build the Set List (Minute 15 through 25)

Display five to ten collected requests on screen and let viewers vote. A poll or survey tool gives you real-time results that are easy to read. Take the top three to five entries and arrange them into your performance order. If you collected ten requests, for instance, rank the top five and lock in the order.

### Phase 3: Perform and Recap (Minute 25 onward)

Sing the selected songs or talk through the chosen topics. After each one, announce the next pick to keep the momentum going. At the end, reflect on your favorite request of the session — a line like "That third-place pick was actually one of my personal favorites too" closes the gap between you and your audience.

## Three Collection Methods — Pick One That Matches Your Scale

The way you gather requests changes how manageable chat feels. Choose based on your typical concurrent viewers.

| Method | Best For | Strength | Watch Out For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Raw chat | Under 50 viewers | Zero setup, casual | Requests scroll away fast |
| Platform poll | Under 200 viewers | Auto-tallied, visual | Must pre-register choices |
| External tool (Streamlabs, etc.) | 100+ viewers | Organized list, spam filters | Requires advance setup |

**1. Raw Chat**

Viewers type something like "Sing [song name]" straight into chat. This is the simplest approach. If you have a moderator on hand, ask them to compile the list in real time. For small streams, this method works on its own.

**2. Platform Poll**

Use YouTube's built-in poll or a similar feature to present choices and tally votes. Pre-loading five to ten candidate songs keeps the flow steady. Because results update live, sharing the poll on screen adds energy to the broadcast.

**3. External Tools**

Services like Streamlabs or dedicated Super Chat readers can manage requests in an organized list. Once you pass 100 concurrent viewers, raw chat becomes hard to track. Tying requests to paid features (like tips or Super Chats) adds another layer of interactivity.

## Tips to Make the Format Your Own

Small production choices can take a request stream from good to great. Here are three ideas you can try today.

1. **Turn request readouts into a mini event.**
   Announce each request with a line like "Next up, this one comes from [viewer name]." Changing your delivery tone or dropping in a drum-roll sound effect makes the reveal feel special.

2. **Deliver ground rules with humor.**
   Instead of a dry "no high-range songs" rule, say something like "If the high notes go above my vocal ceiling, I am running away." Framing limits as jokes keeps the mood light and makes the boundaries memorable.

3. **Add a personal comment to the final set list.**
   When you reveal the chosen songs, drop in a quick reaction — "I was secretly hoping this one would make the cut." Your response to the picks is part of the show.

## Summary

- Split your stream into three phases — collect, vote, perform — for clean, steady pacing.
- Choose your collection method based on audience size: raw chat, platform polls, or external tools.
- Read each request aloud with personality, and frame your rules with humor to make the stream feel uniquely yours.

**Try this next stream:** After your opening greeting, announce a quick request segment. Ask chat for five songs and see what happens. No setup required.
