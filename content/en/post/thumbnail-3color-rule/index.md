You saved the archive, but the thumbnail gets lost in the feed and nobody clicks.
That frustration is common — and the culprit is usually too much information and too many colors.

Narrowing your palette to three colors changes how your thumbnail reads at a glance.
Here is the thinking behind the rule and a practical workflow you can follow.

**What you will learn:**
- Why three is the magic number for color and readability
- How to assign roles — base plus two accents
- A four-step process for building a 3-color thumbnail
- Three ready-made color patterns when you are stuck

---

## Why Three Colors? — What Happens When You Use Too Many

The human eye processes roughly two to three colors in a single glance.
Add more and the brain hesitates — "which part matters?"
Thumbnails appear for a split second. If they do not communicate instantly, they do not get clicked.

Picture a five-color thumbnail: red background, blue title, yellow border, green character, white speech bubble.
It does not look lively — it looks noisy.

Now picture three colors: a deep blue background, the character's pink hair, and white title text.
Less information means viewers know right away: "game stream."

| Color count | Readability | Typical example |
|---|---|---|
| 1-2 | Too plain, feels empty | Logo-only thumbnail |
| 3 | Cohesive, communicates instantly | Game screen + character + text |
| 4 | Slightly busy, borderline acceptable | Seasonal event thumbnails |
| 5+ | Cluttered, unclear focal point | Rainbow event announcements |

Three hits the sweet spot between simple and lively.

## Choosing Your Three Colors — Base + Accent x2

Give each color a clear job.

| Role | Area | Purpose | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Base | 60-70% | Sets the overall mood | Dark blue, brown, deep green |
| Accent 1 | 20-30% | Highlights the main subject | Character's hair, key game element |
| Accent 2 | 5-10% | Draws the eye to key info | White or yellow text, red "NEW" badge |

Pick a **dark base color** whenever you can.
Bright bases make everything on top harder to see.
Dark backgrounds paired with bright elements create strong contrast.

### Game-stream thumbnail example

- Base (65%): Deep green pulled from the game screen
- Accent 1 (25%): Character's orange hair
- Accent 2 (10%): White title text

Even if the game screenshot contains many colors, blurring and darkening the background unifies it as a single base.

### Utawaku (singing stream) thumbnail example

- Base (70%): Deep purple gradient
- Accent 1 (20%): Character's pink outfit
- Accent 2 (10%): Gold song-title text

Singing streams naturally suit dark bases — think night skies, stage lighting, or sunset tones.

## Build It — A 4-Step Workflow

Grab Canva, GIMP, Photoshop, or whatever you prefer and follow these steps.

### Step 1: Pick the base color

Find the **largest-area color** in your game screen or character art.
A horror game? Go with dark blue. An RPG? Pull a warm tone from the environment.
When in doubt, pick something close to black — it rarely fails.

### Step 2: Place the main visual

Drop in your character's standing art, a game screenshot, or both.
Make the visual fill about 50-60% of the frame.
Too small and viewers cannot tell what the stream is about.

### Step 3: Add text in accent color 2

Overlay the stream title or a label like "Singing Stream" or "Gameplay."
Choose a **light color** that contrasts the base — white, yellow, and similar tones work well.
Keep text to roughly 5-10 characters so it stays readable even at small sizes.

### Step 4: Check at 160 px wide

Shrink the finished image to **160 px wide** and look at it.
If the text is readable and the stream type is clear, you are done.
On phones, thumbnails appear roughly this small in archive lists.
If text is unreadable, cut it and let the visual carry the message.

## Three Color Patterns for When You Are Stuck

"Not sure where to start" is a common reaction.
Try one of these three patterns and you will have a palette in seconds.

### Pattern 1: Game-screen based

Pull two colors from a game screenshot.
- Base: Darken the most common color in the screen
- Accent 1: A standout color in the scene (enemy, item, etc.)
- Accent 2: White or yellow text

An ocean game? Deep blue base, white text, orange accent from an on-screen item.

### Pattern 2: Character based

Build from the character's hair and outfit colors.
- Base: The darker tones in the outfit
- Accent 1: Hair color
- Accent 2: White or gold text

Using the character's own colors keeps your branding consistent across thumbnails.
If you have alternate outfits, match the palette to the one you are wearing that day.

### Pattern 3: Season or event based

Lean on seasonal color associations.
- Spring: Cherry-pink + pale green + white
- Summer: Vivid blue + orange + white
- Fall: Dark brown + orange + cream
- Winter: Deep blue + silver + white

Halloween? Purple + orange + white. New Year's? Red + gold + black.
This is the easiest pattern when you want a festive feel without overthinking.

## Recap

- Three colors let viewers grasp the stream topic in a single glance
- Split the palette into base 60-70% plus two accents with clear roles
- Always preview at 160 px wide — if text is unreadable, simplify

Pick one of your current thumbnails and count the colors.
If it uses four or more, drop one and rebuild with three.
That small change can shift how the thumbnail looks in an archive list.
