"I played a horror game on stream but the chat was dead." It happens more than people expect. The game being scary doesn't automatically make the stream entertaining. "Scared streamer" and "entertaining scared streamer" are two different things — and the gap between them is deliberate technique.

This post covers five things you can do to turn horror reactions into content viewers actually enjoy.

**What you'll learn:**
- Why contrast is the engine of horror comedy
- Five concrete techniques with examples you can copy
- How to turn chat into a co-conspirator instead of a silent audience

---

## What Makes a Horror Reaction Actually Entertaining

There are two types of horror stream viewers: those who want to watch you be scared, and those who want to be scared *with* you. Both groups want the same thing — to feel part of the experience.

### Contrast is the mechanism

The reason horror reactions are funny isn't the screaming — it's the *difference* between normal and not-normal. If you're scared the entire time, viewers habituate. The formula is: calm and chatty → sudden quiet → reaction. That swing is what lands.

Dial back your energy between scary moments so the transitions hit harder.

### "In it together" keeps viewers from leaving

When you say "chat is scared too, look at this," you pull viewers into the story rather than leaving them as observers. The chat stops feeling like a scoreboard and starts feeling like a group of people in the same room.

---

## Trick 1 — Camera Size and Position

A camera that's too small in the corner means your face reactions are basically invisible.

### Make your face readable

For horror games, your camera should be large enough to clearly show your eyes and mouth. That's where the reaction lives. If a jump scare fires and the camera is a 100px thumbnail in the corner, it didn't happen.

Use OBS scene switching: one scene with a small camera for exploration, another with a larger camera for tense moments. Set up a keyboard shortcut to switch between them and use it as a deliberate performance choice.

### Face the camera directly

An angled camera makes expressions harder to read. The camera should be roughly level with your face and pointing straight at it. This matters most at the moment of reaction.

---

## Trick 2 — The Pre-Scare Announcement

The scariest part of a horror game isn't the jump scare — it's the moment before it, when you know something is coming but not when.

### Say it out loud

```
"Something is definitely here…"
"That sound has been wrong for a while now…"
"I really don't want to open this door. I really don't want to."
```

Narrating your dread tells chat "we're about to get got" and lets them lean in. The more you build it up, the more satisfying the payoff.

---

## Trick 3 — Adjust Your Audio Mix for Scare Moments

If the game audio is louder than your mic, viewers hear the jump scare and miss your reaction. That's backwards.

### Audio balance by moment

| Moment | Game audio | Mic level |
|---|---|---|
| Exploration | 70–80% | 100% |
| Tense sections | 50–60% | 100% |
| Post-jump-scare | ~40% | Slightly raised |

Set up a second audio profile in OBS for horror games so you're not adjusting on the fly during a scary moment.

---

## Trick 4 — The Three-Second Pause

Yelling continuously after a jump scare buries the peak of your reaction. The better format is: react → stop → comment.

### How it plays out

```
[Jump scare]
"WHAT—"
[3 seconds of silence]
"…okay. My heart is leaving my body."
```

That pause gives chat time to flood in with reactions, which then gives you something to read. The silence isn't dead air — it's the audience's turn. A quiet moment after a big reaction often reads as funnier than extended screaming.

### Going slowly through scary sections

Deliberately walking slowly through a tense area while saying nothing is itself a tension tool. Not speaking can create more suspense than narrating every step.

---

## Trick 5 — Turn Chat into Co-Conspirators

Horror streams are more fun when viewers feel like they're actively participating.

### Use chat as evidence

```
"[Viewer] is scared too — look, they're typing it!"
"'Don't go in there' — okay but I have to go in there"
"Everyone said to open it. Everyone. That's on you all."
```

The last one is especially good: if something bad happens after chat told you to do it, the moment of "I can't believe you all" is a reliable laugh. Chat knows they're setting you up and they love it.

### Let chat vote on your next move

```
"Do I open the door?  Yes → 1  No → 2  Type it in chat"
```

When chat votes "open it" and a monster jumps out, the follow-up — "why did I listen to you people" — is a moment every viewer in that chat feels personally responsible for, in the best way.

---

## Recap

- **Contrast between calm and scared is what makes horror reactions land** — being scared the whole time numbs the effect. Come down between scary moments so the next one hits harder.
- **The three-second pause after a reaction is your cheapest trick** — it gives chat time to pile in, which creates a second wave of energy right after the scare.
- **Making chat responsible for your decisions turns watchers into participants** — "you all told me to do this" is one of the best recurring bits in horror streaming.
