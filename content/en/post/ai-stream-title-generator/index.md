Do you catch yourself using the same stream title pattern every time?

For zatsudan (casual free-talk streams) it ends up something like "Chill Chat - Let's Hang." For games, "First Playthrough of [Title]." Falling into a routine is natural. But when every archive looks the same, viewers have a hard time telling them apart.

This post walks you through **having AI draft ten title candidates, then picking the best one with an A/B test**.

**What you will learn:**

- Why titles get repetitive and how AI fixes that
- A ready-to-use prompt template for generating titles
- A five-point checklist to vet AI output, plus how to run a simple A/B test

---

## Why stream titles get stale

Coming up with a title is one of those quiet time-sinks in stream prep. Five minutes before go-live, you think "whatever, I will just use last week's" and copy-paste. Sound familiar?

Three things drive this rut:

1. **"Good enough" has no clear definition** — if you can't explain why a title works, you default to what you used before
2. **Writing ten variations is a lot of work** — doing it from scratch right before a stream feels like too much
3. **Old titles feel safe** — unconsciously, you avoid the risk of changing something that hasn't caused problems

AI helps by cutting the effort and the perceived risk. Choosing from a list is a lot easier than writing that list yourself.

**Example:** Writing ten zatsudan titles by hand can take 15-20 minutes. With a good prompt, AI gives you ten options in about 30 seconds. Including pick time, you are done in roughly five minutes.

## A prompt template that actually works

When you ask AI for titles, include three essentials in the prompt: **stream genre, target audience, and character limit**. Skip any of these and the results won't match your stream.

### Base template

```
You are a stream-title brainstorming assistant for a VTuber.
Suggest 10 titles based on the following:

- Genre: [chat / gaming / utawaku (singing stream) / etc.]
- Content: [1-2 lines about what you plan to do]
- Audience: [new viewers / regulars / both]
- Length: [under 30 chars / under 50 chars / etc.]
- Vibe: [energetic / calm / slightly provocative / etc.]

Output format:
1. [title]
2. [title]
... (10 total)
```

The key detail here is the **"vibe" field**. Adding a word that matches your on-stream personality — "bubbly," "cozy," "snarky" — pulls the output closer to your style.

### Genre-specific examples

Plug in real details and here is what you get.

**Chat stream**

```
- Genre: chat
- Content: sharing recent obsessions + viewer Q&A corner
- Audience: both
- Length: under 40 chars
- Vibe: friendly and casual
```

**Gaming stream**

```
- Genre: gaming
- Content: first-time indie horror playthrough
- Audience: new viewers
- Length: under 35 chars
- Vibe: bold and exciting
```

**Utawaku (singing stream)**

```
- Genre: utawaku
- Content: 90s J-POP set, viewer requests welcome
- Audience: regulars
- Length: under 30 chars
- Vibe: warm and soothing
```

Changing just the conditions shifts the flavor of the titles. Copy this template, swap in your own details, and give it a try.

## Do not use AI output as-is — run this checklist first

AI titles are handy, but **always review them before publishing**. AI sometimes misreads context or produces awkward phrasing.

Only keep titles that pass these five checks.

### Five-point checklist

1. **Fits your persona** — would you actually say those words on stream? Drop anything that sounds off-brand
2. **Fits mobile search results** — YouTube on phones shows roughly 40 characters, so front-load the important part
3. **Includes searchable keywords** — terms like "first playthrough," "requests open," or a game name help viewers find you
4. **No misleading claims** — "Pro Gamer Plays" or "Cleared Every Song" had better be true, or viewers will distrust you
5. **Not a generic copy** — template-sounding titles get buried. Look for something that stands out

### What to do with the rejects

Titles that fail the checklist are not garbage — treat them as idea seeds.

**Example:** Suppose AI suggests "The Horror Game That Will Make You Cry." "Will make you cry" is over-the-top, but the emotional hook is useful. Edit it to "First Horror Playthrough — Will There Be Tears?" and you have a title that sounds like you.

Think of AI as a starting point, not the final answer.

## Running a simple A/B test

Once you have two or three solid titles from AI, compare them with an A/B test. The process is straightforward.

### Four steps

1. **Pick two titles from AI** — choose two that pass the checklist but take different angles
2. **Stream the same format twice** — same genre, same day of week, same time slot; only the title changes
3. **Compare archive views after 48 hours** — wait two full days before you judge, since live-viewer counts fluctuate a lot
4. **Log the results in a spreadsheet** — columns: date, title A, title B, view count at 48 h

### Example tracking sheet

| Date | Title A | Title B | What changed | Views (48 h) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 4/22 | Chat - Hanging Out Again | Chat - Ranting About My New Obsession | closing phrasing | 180 / 260 |
| 4/29 | Gaming - First Horror Playthrough | Gaming - Can I Survive This Horror Game? | wording + punctuation | 310 / 420 |

**Run at least five rounds** before you draw conclusions. One or two tests have too much random noise. Testing the same genre on the same day each week gives you reliable data in about five weeks.

### Keep everything else constant

The biggest reason A/B tests break down is that something besides the title changed. Watch out for:

- Start time — keep it within a **30-minute window**
- Thumbnail — use the **same template**
- Game — play the same title

If conditions have to shift, jot it down in a "notes" column. A comment like "collab stream this week" saves you confusion later.

## Summary

- Generating ten titles with AI cuts your brainstorming time down to **about five minutes**
- Include **genre, content, audience, character limit, and vibe** in every prompt
- Vet AI output with the **five-point checklist**, then confirm the winner with an A/B test

**What to try tonight:**

1. Copy the prompt template above and fill in your stream's genre, content, and vibe
2. Filter the ten results through the checklist, then pick two or three finalists
3. On your next stream of the same genre, swap in a different title and compare 48-hour views

Start with step one. Even just prompting AI and picking from the list should keep your title workflow under five minutes.
