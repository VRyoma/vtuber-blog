Ever freeze up mid-stream with no idea what to say next? Some streams flow naturally. Others hit a ten-minute silence wall. Preparing a script sounds like the fix — but writing one from scratch every time is its own chore.

Hand AI your stream type, your main topic, and one question for chat, and it returns a working rundown in under two minutes. This post walks through the prompts and how to actually use what comes back.

**What you'll get from this:**
- The three moments where ad-lib most often breaks down
- Ready-to-copy prompts for chat, game, and karaoke streams
- Three quick edits that make the AI output sound like you

---

## Where Ad-Lib Breaks Down

Going in with no plan works — until it doesn't. Three specific moments make ad-lib especially hard. Knowing them tells you exactly where a short script pays off.

### The first five minutes

Early in a stream, chat is sparse and comments are slow. A nervous "uh, so, today we're gonna..." sets the energy for everything that follows.

A three-point opener — greeting, what you're doing, one question for chat — takes about 30 seconds to write in advance and makes the start feel deliberate.

### Dead air during loads and game-overs

Game streams have gaps: loading screens, death animations, cutscenes. A 10-to-30-second silence with nothing to say can push viewers to click away.

Three talking points prepared for those gaps is all it takes to keep the energy up.

### The ending that just stops

Cutting to the end card with a mumbled "okay, that's it, thanks" leaves a weak final impression. The closing is actually where a script helps most — a quick next-stream mention, a thank-you, one line for chat.

## The Core Prompt Structure

Every AI stream script prompt needs three things: stream type, main event, and a question for chat. Those three inputs are enough for AI to return a usable draft in one to two minutes.

### The base template

Copy this directly and fill in the brackets.

```
You are a VTuber stream assistant.
Using the information below, create a stream rundown for today.

- Stream type: [chat stream / game commentary / karaoke / other]
- Main event: [e.g. first playthrough of a new game]
- Question for chat: [e.g. what game have you been into lately?]
- Stream length: [e.g. 1 hour]

Output format:
1. Opening (3-4 lines)
2. Main content talking points (5-7 bullet points)
3. Filler line options for gaps (2-3 examples)
4. Closing (3-4 lines)
```

### How to read the output

Treat what AI returns as a list of waypoints, not a word-for-word script. Before you go live, check two things:

- Does it sound like something you'd actually say?
- Is there at least one question for chat?

If both are true, you're ready. Glance at it between segments — you don't need to read every word.

## Prompts by Stream Type

Here are three ready-to-copy prompts. Swap out the bracketed parts.

### Chat stream

```
You are a VTuber stream assistant.
Today I'm running a one-hour chat stream.

Topic: [e.g. things I bought recently that I regret]
Question for chat: [e.g. what's something you impulse-bought lately?]
Side story I want to tell: [e.g. something weird I found at a convenience store]

Create:
1. An opening line (friendly, 1-2 sentences)
2. Topic flow (3-4 steps)
3. A suggested moment to open the floor to chat
4. A closing line
```

### Game commentary

```
You are a VTuber stream assistant.
Today I'm playing [game name].

Play status: [e.g. first playthrough / replay / boss-only run]
Highlight moment: [e.g. the stage 3 boss / first clear attempt]

Create:
1. Game intro (assume viewers don't know it, 2-3 sentences)
2. Commentary talking points during play (5 points, one line each)
3. Filler topics for load screens (3 options)
4. Outro (include a next-stream mention)
```

### Karaoke stream

*Karaoke streams (歌枠, utawaku) are a VTuber staple — a live singing session where the streamer performs songs, often with chat requests.*

```
You are a VTuber stream assistant.
Today I'm running a [length]-minute karaoke stream.

Theme: [e.g. anime OPs only / nostalgic J-pop]
Set list: [3-5 song titles]

Create:
1. Opening talk (around 30 seconds when spoken aloud)
2. MC comments before and after each song (1-2 lines per song)
3. A line for taking song requests from chat
4. Closing
```

## Three Edits That Make It Sound Like You

Reading AI output verbatim sounds stiff. Three targeted edits fix that.

### 1. Swap endings to match how you actually talk

AI defaults to formal, neutral language. Replace sentence-endings with phrases you actually use on stream. Three to five swaps shifts the whole tone.

### 2. Cut chat questions down to one

AI tends to scatter multiple "let me ask chat" moments through the script. Five check-ins in an hour breaks the flow. Keep the one most likely to get a good response and delete the rest.

### 3. Use the headers — ignore the body text

If you try to read every line, you'll be looking away from the camera the whole time. In practice, the section headers are all you need as anchors: Opening, Game Intro, Boss Fight, Outro. Use the body text to plan, then let it go once you're live.

| Section | AI output | Your version |
|---|---|---|
| Opener | "Hello everyone, thank you for joining today's stream!" | "Hey hey, glad you're here!" |
| Close | "That concludes today's broadcast. Thank you for watching." | "Thanks for hanging out — see you next time!" |

## The 2-Minute Feedback Loop

After each stream, spend two minutes sending AI this note. Do it five or six times and the template will start fitting your style almost automatically.

```
What worked from the last script: [e.g. the game intro felt natural]
What didn't work: [e.g. the load-screen fillers ran too long]
Change for next time: [e.g. cut filler options to one, keep it short]
```

---

## Summary

- **Ad-lib breaks most often at three points: the opener, dead air, and the outro.** A short script for just those moments covers the biggest gaps.
- **Give AI your stream type, main event, and a chat question** — you'll have a working draft in under ten minutes.
- **Use the output as section headers, not lines to read.** Edit the endings to sound like you, keep one chat question, and you're ready to go.
